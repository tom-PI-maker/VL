## Section 1: ADHD Medications and Cardiac Side Effects

### Comment 1: Who actually needs cardiology input before starting a stimulant?
**🟡 REFINE**

**Gap**: The script says "consider cardiology consultation" for high-risk patients and "baseline ECG is recommended despite limited predictive value." A clinician is left unsure: do I send every cardiac patient to cardiology, or only some? What's the threshold?

**Question for faculty**: Could you sketch the patient profile for which you would *not* start an ADHD stimulant without a cardiology opinion first — versus the profile for which a baseline BP/HR and ECG in your own clinic are enough?

**Suggested answer** *(draft for faculty review)*:

> A pragmatic threshold I use:
>
> - **Start in clinic (baseline BP, HR, ECG in office):** adult with well-controlled hypertension on a stable regimen, no history of arrhythmia or structural heart disease, resting HR < 100 bpm, BP < 140/90, normal baseline ECG (no LVH, no QTc prolongation).
> - **Cardiology input before starting:** any history of arrhythmia (especially atrial fibrillation or supraventricular tachycardia), known structural heart disease, prior MI or revascularisation, heart failure, unexplained syncope, a strong family history of sudden cardiac death under 40, or baseline QTc > 460 ms (men) / 470 ms (women).
> - **Avoid stimulants altogether if possible:** active unstable angina, recent MI (< 3 months), uncontrolled hypertension, or a known proarrhythmic channelopathy.
>
> The documentation point matters: when prescribing outside manufacturer cautions, record the risk–benefit discussion, the alternatives considered, and the monitoring plan agreed with the patient.

### Comment 2: Which non-stimulant alternatives do you actually reach for?
**🟡 REFINE**

**Gap**: The script says "use non-drug options or other medication in preference" in cardiovascular disease, but doesn't name the alternatives. Atomoxetine is named as sympathomimetic (so not an escape). A clinician is left asking: what do I try instead?

**Question for faculty**: When stimulants are off the table for a cardiac patient, what is your practical hierarchy — atomoxetine anyway (at lower doses), viloxazine, bupropion, guanfacine/clonidine, modafinil off-label? Could you rank the options and say a word about each?

**Suggested answer** *(draft for faculty review)*:

> When stimulants are not the right choice in cardiac disease:
>
> 1. **Non-drug first** — CBT for adult ADHD, coaching, workplace accommodations. Often insufficient as monotherapy for moderate–severe ADHD but should be offered.
> 2. **Atomoxetine at cautious doses** — still sympathomimetic, but the BP/HR signal is generally smaller than with stimulants and there is no euphoriant effect, so it can be a reasonable option with monitoring.
> 3. **Guanfacine (extended release) or clonidine** — actually *lower* blood pressure, which can be an advantage in hypertensive patients. A reasonable option particularly when stimulants are contraindicated.
> 4. **Bupropion** — off-label for ADHD, avoid in patients with a proarrhythmic substrate or seizure risk, but can be useful when there is co-occurring depression.
>
> Viloxazine is newer and I have less experience with it; the cardiovascular signal appears similar to atomoxetine.

**Gap**: Baseline ECG is addressed, but the script doesn't specify how often to recheck BP/HR once the patient is stable on a stimulant.

**Question for faculty**: Does this match your practice — BP and HR at every dose change, then every 3 months for the first year, then 6–12 monthly once stable?

**Suggested answer** *(draft for faculty review)*:

> Standard cadence:
>
> - BP and HR at baseline and at every dose titration visit.
> - Then every 3 months for the first year.
> - Then 6–12 monthly if stable, lower threshold to recheck if dose changes or a new cardiovascular risk appears.
> - Repeat ECG only if new symptoms (palpitations, chest pain, syncope) develop — routine interval ECGs are not supported.

## Section 2: Anticoagulation — Focus on Antidepressants

### Comment 4: Mirtazapine vs. agomelatine — which one first?
**🟡 REFINE**

**Gap**: The script says "first choice: mirtazapine or agomelatine" but doesn't help the clinician choose between them. Mirtazapine has the warfarin INR caveat and weight gain; agomelatine is a safer alternative per the text. A clinician reading this is left unsure which to start.

**Question for faculty**: In your own practice, when a patient on warfarin or a NOAC needs an antidepressant, do you go to agomelatine first or mirtazapine first? And what tilts the choice?

**Suggested answer** *(draft for faculty review)*:

> My default hierarchy for an anticoagulated patient:
>
> - **Agomelatine first** when available and liver function is normal — no INR signal, no bleeding signal, weight neutral, sleep benefit without daytime sedation. Requires LFTs at baseline, 3, 6, 12, and 24 weeks.
> - **Mirtazapine** when agomelatine is unavailable (as it is in the US), when insomnia or weight loss is prominent, or when LFTs preclude agomelatine. If the patient is on warfarin, add an INR check 5–7 days after starting and after any dose change.
> - **Sertraline** remains an option when the bleeding risk is judged low and the depression is more severe — but I would discuss and document the small incremental bleeding risk.
>
> Agomelatine is not available in the US or Canada — worth flagging for international audiences.

### Comment 5: INR monitoring schedule when starting mirtazapine on warfarin
**🟢 CONFIRM**

**Gap**: The script says "monitor INR at initiation and dose changes" but no interval is given. Clinicians want a concrete number.

**Question for faculty**: Does this match your practice? INR 5–7 days after starting mirtazapine, again 1 week after any dose increase, and return to routine schedule once stable.

**Suggested answer** *(draft for faculty review)*:

> For a patient stable on warfarin who starts mirtazapine:
>
> - Check INR 5–7 days after the first mirtazapine dose.
> - Recheck 1 week after any mirtazapine dose increase.
> - Return to the patient's routine warfarin clinic schedule once stable over two consecutive measurements.
> - If the INR rises unexpectedly, treat this as a warfarin dose-adjustment event and inform the anticoagulation service — the interaction is real but uncommon.

### Comment 6: Where do vortioxetine, vilazodone, and bupropion sit?
**🟡 REFINE**

**Gap**: The script's list of "preferred low-risk antidepressants" is drawn largely from older agents (trazodone, mianserin, reboxetine, TCAs, moclobemide). Clinicians in many markets reach for newer agents — vortioxetine, vilazodone, bupropion — and may want to know whether these are alternatives.

**Question for faculty**: Could you say a word about whether vortioxetine, vilazodone, or bupropion have a place in anticoagulated patients? Bupropion has no serotonergic bleeding mechanism; vortioxetine does bind SERT but the clinical bleeding data are thinner.

**Suggested answer** *(draft for faculty review)*:

> Brief positioning of the newer options:
>
> - **Bupropion** — no SERT affinity, no meaningful bleeding signal. A reasonable choice in anticoagulated patients without a seizure or proarrhythmic substrate, and has the advantage of being weight-neutral.
> - **Vortioxetine** — a high-affinity SERT ligand; mechanism predicts bleeding risk comparable to SSRIs, and the real-world data are limited. I would not position it ahead of mirtazapine or agomelatine in an anticoagulated patient.
> - **Vilazodone** — also SERT-active; similar cautions as SSRIs. Not a first choice.

## Section 3: Anticoagulation — Focus on Antipsychotics

### Comment 7: What if the patient is already stable on quetiapine?
**🟡 REFINE**

**Gap**: The script recommends avoiding haloperidol and quetiapine in patients on NOACs. But many patients present *already stable* on quetiapine for years. A clinician needs to know: do I switch everyone, or only in specific situations?

**Question for faculty**: When a patient who is psychiatrically stable on quetiapine starts a NOAC, would you automatically switch the antipsychotic, or would you continue and monitor? What is the threshold for action?

**Suggested answer** *(draft for faculty review)*:

> I would not automatically switch a psychiatrically stable patient. My approach:
>
> - **Stable on quetiapine for years, low bleeding risk, low quetiapine dose (e.g., ≤ 300 mg for sleep/anxiety in depression):** continue, counsel on bleeding warning signs, consider whether NOAC dose needs to sit at the lower end of its range.
> - **Stable on quetiapine for psychosis at higher doses and on a NOAC:** discuss with the anticoagulation team, look for additional bleeding risk factors (renal impairment, age, concurrent antiplatelet), and consider a cautious switch to olanzapine or aripiprazole only if those factors are stacking up.
> - **Starting an antipsychotic *de novo* in a patient already on a NOAC:** olanzapine or aripiprazole is the default; reserve quetiapine for a clear indication that the alternatives cannot meet.
>
> Switching a stable patient carries its own relapse risk and should not be done reflexively on the basis of a pharmacokinetic signal from cohort data.

### Comment 8: Delirium in the elderly anticoagulated patient — what replaces haloperidol?
**🟡 REFINE**

**Gap**: The script flags haloperidol as higher-risk for NOAC interactions and mentions the elderly dementia/delirium context specifically — but doesn't say what to use instead in acute delirium on a medical ward, where haloperidol is the most widely used tool.

**Question for faculty**: In an elderly patient on a NOAC who develops hyperactive delirium on a medical ward, what would you suggest to the medical team as a safer alternative — and at what dose?

**Suggested answer** *(draft for faculty review)*:

> Practical options in hyperactive delirium for an elderly patient on a NOAC:
>
> - **Non-pharmacological first** — reorientation, family presence, treating the underlying cause, ensuring hearing aids/glasses, addressing pain and constipation. This is not a platitude; much delirium responds without antipsychotics.
> - **Olanzapine 2.5 mg PO/ODT** as needed, up to 5–10 mg daily if tolerated — lower CYP3A4 involvement, less interaction signal, orodispersible formulation is useful for the resistant patient.
> - **Aripiprazole 5 mg** is an alternative with the lowest metabolic and QT profile, though it can be activating.
> - **Avoid quetiapine** for this indication unless there is a strong reason (e.g., severe parkinsonism).
> - **Benzodiazepines only for alcohol withdrawal delirium** — they worsen most other deliriums.
>
> Duration should be the shortest possible, with daily review and a clear stop date.

### Comment 9: VTE prophylaxis when starting an antipsychotic
**🟢 CONFIRM**

**Gap**: The script flags the paradoxical VTE signal (4 extra cases/10,000 person-years, 10 for 65+) but offers no guidance on who, if anyone, needs VTE prophylaxis when starting an antipsychotic.

**Question for faculty**: Can you confirm your practice matches this — no routine prophylaxis, but lower threshold for LMWH in an inpatient on new antipsychotic who has additional VTE risk factors (immobility, obesity, malignancy, recent surgery)?

**Suggested answer** *(draft for faculty review)*:

> The absolute VTE risk from antipsychotics is small and does not justify routine chemical prophylaxis on starting one. Practical approach:
>
> - For an *inpatient* started on a new antipsychotic who has any additional VTE risk factor (reduced mobility, BMI ≥ 30, active cancer, recent surgery, age ≥ 65), apply the standard ward VTE prophylaxis protocol — the antipsychotic is not a contraindication.
> - For an *outpatient*, encourage mobility and hydration; counsel on VTE warning symptoms; no prophylaxis unless the medical history already calls for it.
> - Address modifiable risk factors that the antipsychotic itself creates — principally weight gain — with dietary review and activity support.

## Section 4: Anticoagulation — Focus on Mood Stabilizers

### Comment 10: The patient already on valproate who starts an anticoagulant
**🟡 REFINE**

**Gap**: The script says "avoid valproate" and "lithium or lamotrigine preferred," but many patients are already long-term stable on valproate when they develop an indication for anticoagulation (AF, DVT, post-MI). The script doesn't help the clinician decide whether to switch.

**Question for faculty**: For a psychiatrically stable patient on valproate who now needs a NOAC or warfarin, what's your threshold for switching the mood stabilizer versus continuing with monitoring?

**Suggested answer** *(draft for faculty review)*:

> The decision is rarely automatic. My checklist:
>
> - **How stable is the patient on valproate?** Years of stability on valproate, especially for bipolar with prior severe episodes, weighs heavily against switching.
> - **Which anticoagulant?** Warfarin is easier to co-manage — INR is monitorable. With a NOAC, the valproate interaction is theoretical (reduced NOAC level via CYP3A4/PgP induction) and hard to measure; if this route is taken, discuss with the anticoagulation team.
> - **Baseline hematology** — check platelets, fibrinogen, and PT/APTT before and during combined therapy.
> - **Switch trigger** — any clinically significant bleeding, heavy menstrual bleeding, unexplained thrombocytopenia, or a failed anticoagulation (suggesting reduced NOAC levels due to induction).
> - **If switching** — to lithium if tolerable (watch renal function if the cardiac condition has compromised it) or lamotrigine if the index episode was depressive.

## Section 5: Dialysis — Generalities and Antidepressants

### Comment 13: Sertraline vs. mirtazapine — the tie-breaker
**🟡 REFINE**

**Gap**: Both are "preferred" for dialysis. The script gives pharmacokinetic rationale but no clinical decision rule. A clinician wants a simple tie-breaker.

**Question for faculty**: In a dialysis patient with depression and no other distinguishing features, do you default to sertraline or mirtazapine? And which patient features tilt you toward the other?

**Suggested answer** *(draft for faculty review)*:

> My default tie-breaker:
>
> - **Sertraline** for most cases — no dose adjustment needed in ESRD, strong safety data post-MI (SADHART), easy to titrate.
> - **Mirtazapine** when the patient has prominent insomnia, weight loss, early morning waking, anxious agitation, or uremic pruritus (histamine blockade can help). Start 7.5 mg nocte given the ~50% clearance reduction, increase cautiously.
> - **Avoid both together** routinely — the combination adds little for most dialysis patients and stacks sedation and weight gain.

### Comment 14: Already on citalopram when dialysis starts — switch, or not?
**🟡 REFINE**

**Gap**: The script says "avoid citalopram and escitalopram" but doesn't address the common real-world case: a patient was already stable on citalopram when they transitioned to dialysis.

**Question for faculty**: Would you switch every such patient, or only those with additional QT risk factors (other QT-prolonging meds, bradyarrhythmia, female sex, hypokalemia)?

**Suggested answer** *(draft for faculty review)*:

> I would not switch reflexively. My approach:
>
> - **Check a baseline ECG** at the start of dialysis — QTc, rate, rhythm.
> - **Review the total QT risk** — any other QT-prolonging medications (ondansetron, fluconazole, amiodarone, methadone), electrolyte shifts, bradyarrhythmia, female sex, age > 65.
> - **If QTc is normal and there are no stacked risks**: continue citalopram, recheck ECG at any symptom change or new QT-prolonging medication, and reassess annually.
> - **If QTc is borderline or any risk factor is present**: switch to sertraline, cross-tapered over 2–4 weeks.
> - **The reported absolute risk** is about 1 extra sudden cardiac death per 48 patient-years at risk — small but not trivial, and cumulative over years on dialysis.

### Comment 16: Paliperidone dose reduction — how to titrate from half-dose
**🟢 CONFIRM**

**Gap**: "Reduce by at least half initially" for paliperidone in dialysis — but then what? Titrate up based on symptoms? Plasma concentrations? No follow-through.

**Question for faculty**: For oral paliperidone, starting at ≥50% dose reduction — do you then titrate upward based on clinical response only, or do you use plasma concentrations? For the depot, is the starting dose the next step down on the available strengths (e.g., 100 mg → 50 mg)?

**Suggested answer** *(draft for faculty review)*:

> My practical approach in dialysis:
>
> - **Oral paliperidone**: halve the previous effective dose, give after dialysis, reassess clinical response every 2 weeks, titrate cautiously in 3 mg increments. Plasma concentrations are useful if relapse or toxicity is suspected.
> - **Paliperidone palmitate depot (monthly)**: step down to the next strength (e.g., 100 mg → 50 mg), review after 2 cycles, watch for relapse signs and EPS/prolactin effects.
> - **All doses given after dialysis**, not before — though since it accumulates slowly and is not dialyzed meaningfully, the timing matters less than it does for lithium.

## Section 7: Dialysis — Lithium, Valproate, and Lamotrigine

### Comment 17: A simpler bedside lithium rule?
**🟡 REFINE**

**Gap**: The Bjarnson formula is mathematically rigorous but intimidating at the bedside. Many clinicians would benefit from a practical "first-pass" rule.

**Question for faculty**: Is the simpler rule — 200–400 mg after dialysis only, check pre-dialysis level before the next session, adjust in 100–200 mg increments — your actual bedside approach, reserving the formula for atypical body sizes or complex cases?

**Suggested answer** *(draft for faculty review)*:

> Practical bedside approach:
>
> 1. **First dose**: 300 mg immediate-release lithium carbonate after the first dialysis session. (200 mg if frail or low body weight; 400 mg if larger body habitus.)
> 2. **No doses on non-dialysis days** unless significant residual urine output.
> 3. **Check lithium level before the next dialysis session** (pre-dialysis trough) — this is your target, aim for the lower end of therapeutic (0.4–0.6 mmol/L initially).
> 4. **Adjust** in 100–200 mg increments based on pre-dialysis level.
> 5. **Daily pre-dialysis levels for the first 2 weeks**, then dialysis-day only, then 1–3 times monthly once stable on three consecutive levels.
> 6. Use the Bjarnson formula when the patient is unusually small, unusually large, or when the simpler rule is struggling to reach a stable level.
>
> Also: use standard serum tubes, not lithium heparin. A single mislabeled tube can cost the patient a hospitalization.

### Comment 18: Target valproate level in dialysis
**🟢 CONFIRM**

**Gap**: "Monitor plasma concentrations" for valproate is repeated but no target is given. Clinicians want a number.

**Question for faculty**: Is your target for valproate in dialysis the same as in a non-dialysis patient (50–100 mg/L total), or do you aim lower given the increased free fraction from low albumin?

**Suggested answer** *(draft for faculty review)*:

> Because uremia reduces protein binding, total valproate levels underestimate the active free fraction in dialysis patients. Practical approach:
>
> - **Target a total level at the lower end of therapeutic (50–75 mg/L)** and interpret cautiously.
> - **If free valproate assay is available**, use it — target 6–10 mg/L.
> - **Watch clinically for toxicity** (sedation, tremor, ataxia) more than for the number — the case in Section 9 is a good illustration.
> - **Give the dose after dialysis** and recheck levels if dose changes or symptoms appear.

### Comment 19: Is there a "target level" for lamotrigine in dialysis?
**🟡 REFINE**

**Gap**: The script says "give after dialysis and monitor plasma concentrations" for lamotrigine, but lamotrigine has no established therapeutic range in psychiatric use. A clinician trying to follow this recommendation won't know what to do with the number.

**Question for faculty**: What role, if any, does lamotrigine level monitoring actually play in a dialysis patient? Is it more about trend (to detect accumulation or under-dosing) than about a target range?

**Suggested answer** *(draft for faculty review)*:

> Lamotrigine has no established therapeutic range for bipolar maintenance, so "monitoring levels" is more about:
>
> - **Baseline reference** — get a level once the patient is clinically stable on the dose you want.
> - **Troubleshooting**: a new side effect (rash, ataxia, dizziness) or loss of efficacy is a reason to recheck the level and compare to the patient's own baseline.
> - **Not a dose-titration tool** in most cases — clinical response remains primary.
>
> Because the inactive glucuronide metabolite accumulates in renal impairment but the parent drug does not, most patients on dialysis do not need dose adjustment for lamotrigine.
