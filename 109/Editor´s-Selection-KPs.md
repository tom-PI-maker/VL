Section 1: “Updated Psychopharmacology Algorithm for PTSD”

Sequence treatment sleep-first: 70-90% of PTSD patients have insomnia, and prazosin has the strongest evidence for sleep-maintenance disturbance.
Only paroxetine and sertraline are FDA-approved for PTSD. Consider SSRIs after addressing sleep disturbances.
In PTSD with psychosis, add aripiprazole only if psychotic symptoms persist after an SSRI trial. These symptoms often remit with treatment of the whole syndrome.

Section 2: “Differential Diagnosis of PTSD: The Importance of Comorbidities”

Target prazosin to patients with comorbid PTSD and AUD who exhibit hyperadrenergic features, particularly hypertension. This subgroup has demonstrated benefit for both drinking outcomes and PTSD symptoms.
Assess every PTSD patient for bipolar disorder. If positive, skip antidepressant steps in the algorithm.
In PTSD during pregnancy, consider sertraline. Paroxetine has been associated with fetal cardiac septal defects.

Section 3: “Sleep Disturbance Assessment: The First Step in PTSD Treatment”

When assessing sleep disturbances in PTSD, count disturbed awakenings as nightmares at baseline. This is a key symptom of PTSD-related sleep problems and a target for treatment.
Investigate nicotine withdrawal in pack-a-day smokers who awaken after several hours. A key indicator is the patient’s ability to return to sleep after smoking a cigarette.
For sleep terrors with thrashing and screaming, advise bed partners not to shake the patient awake due to risk of assault.

Section 4: Prazosin for PTSD-Related Nightmares: Rationale and Dosing Strategies
For PTSD nightmares, consider prazosin first-line. Add a sleep-onset agent if initial insomnia is prominent, because prazosin is non-sedating. 
Before prescribing prazosin, screen for a history of hypertension. Then, adjust existing antihypertensives to prevent hypotension.
Prazosin dosing is typically higher in men than in women. Mean final doses are 16 mg at night and 5 mg in the morning for men, compared to 7 mg at night and 2 mg in the morning for women.

Section 5: Pharmacological Management of Sleep Initiation Disturbances in PTSD
Use hydroxyzine first-line and trazodone second-line for sleep-onset insomnia in PTSD, once prazosin has addressed nightmares and awakenings.
Consider clonidine if sedation and sleep maintenance are needed. Dose up to 0.5 mg nightly; 0.05 mg daytime up to TID as needed.
Assess for conditioned insomnia when fear of nightmares persists. If identified, prioritize CBT-I over escalating hypnotic medication.

Section 6: Undesirable Initial Choices for Sleep Management in PTSD

Avoid using quetiapine first-line for any insomnia. Benefits fail to justify weight, metabolic, cardiac, and TD risks.
Do not prescribe amitriptyline for PTSD-related sleep despite efficacy data. Worst-in-class burden: anticholinergic, alpha-adrenergic blockade, sexual dysfunction, sedation, and arrhythmia risk.
Benzodiazepines interfere with exposure therapy in PTSD. They impair both short- and long-term learning, undermining trauma-focused psychotherapy.

Section 7: Assessment and Management of Non-Sleep Symptoms: Using SSRIs

Counsel patients that SSRI efficacy in PTSD is modest. Pooled meta-analyses show response rates roughly 20–25% higher than placebo.
Although paroxetine and sertraline are FDA-approved for PTSD, consider escitalopram for its favorable tolerability profile.
Warn patients about insomnia, sexual dysfunction in 30-60%, and increased GI bleeding risk when combined with NSAIDs.

Section 8: Psychopharmacological Approaches to PTSD with Psychotic Features

Risperidone has the strongest RCT evidence for PTSD with psychotic features. A dedicated trial targeted this subpopulation specifically.
Prefer aripiprazole for antipsychotic augmentation as it has fewer side effects than risperidone or quetiapine. Start at 2–5 mg and titrate up to an average of 12 mg.
Consider quetiapine at doses of 25–800 mg for re-experiencing, hyperarousal, and psychosis. Alpha-1 antagonism may help with sleep.


Section 9: PTSD Pharmacotherapy: Management of Non-Response to Initial SSRI Trial
Do not rely on venlafaxine for hyperarousal, insomnia, or PTSD-related nightmares. Its noradrenergic stimulation likely worsens these symptoms.
Start duloxetine at 30 mg and titrate toward 120 mg maximum. Expect faster onset in PTSD with comorbid depression or anxiety.
Use mirtazapine as SSRI augmentation. A monotherapy RCT showed no difference compared with placebo.


Section 10: Third-Line Therapeutic Options for PTSD

Trial another SSRI or SNRI not yet used before moving to adrenergic agents. Note that neither works very well for hyperarousal.
After two failed antidepressants, offer daytime prazosin as the primary third-line option for persistent daytime hyperarousal and irritability.
Consider daytime clonidine as an alternative third-line option, supported by retrospective data.

Section 11: Psychopharmacological Strategies for Treatment-Resistant PTSD: Augmentation

Among anticonvulsants: reserve lamotrigine for comorbid bipolar depression with PTSD, and try topiramate around 300 mg as a last step in the algorithm.
Drop doxazosin as a prazosin alternative. A 2023 trial with drinkers failed to improve their PTSD. 
Cannabis RCT showed no benefit for PTSD. All three THC and CBD concentrations performed equivalently to placebo.

Section 12: THM
Prescribe prazosin as a first-line agent for sleep disturbances in PTSD. Ensure correct dosing, and monitor for postural hypotension and dizziness.
Reserve SSRIs for use after antiadrenergic agents. Prefer escitalopram; paroxetine carries a higher burden of sexual dysfunction and weight gain.
Counsel patients against using cannabis for PTSD. Current evidence suggests it is ineffective and potentially harmful.
