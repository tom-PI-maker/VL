# VL109 — Psychopharmacology Algorithm for PTSD
## Selected Key Points — All 12 Sections

**Faculty:** David Osser, M.D.

---

## Section 1 — Algorithm Introduction

1. **Sequence treatment sleep-first: 70-90% of PTSD patients have insomnia, and prazosin has the strongest evidence for sleep-maintenance disturbance.**
   > *"led us to actually propose a treatment sequence that starts with treating sleep disturbance first usually with prazosin"*

2. **Add daytime prazosin for residual hyperarousal at step three; evidence is moderate but underused. Otherwise rotate SSRI/SNRI.**
   > *"you could try prazosin in the daytime. It's not terribly well established as a daytime treatment but there is a moderate amount of evidence"*

3. **In PTSD with psychosis, trial an SSRI first since psychotic symptoms often remit with whole-syndrome treatment; add aripiprazole only if they persist.**
   > *"we recommend adding an antipsychotic, probably aripiprazole is the recommendation. Notice that we did recommend an SSRI even if they had psychosis to be tried first"*

---

## Section 2 — Comorbidities

1. **Target prazosin to PTSD plus AUD patients showing hyperadrenergic features, especially hypertension; this sub-cohort benefited for drinking and PTSD.**
   > *"benefit in preventing drinking in a subgroup of drinkers who had PTSD and high blood pressure which Dr. Raskind called the hyperadrenergic sub-cohort"*

2. **For PTSD with psychotic features, skip the sleep phase. Start an SSRI and add aripiprazole if SSRI fails.**
   > *"we would consider skipping the sleep phase of the algorithm and move more directly to an SSRI and if that doesn't work to adding an antipsychotic"*

3. **Avoid paroxetine and fluoxetine in pregnancy. Use sertraline. Paroxetine is associated with fetal cardiac septal defects.**
   > *"we certainly don't want to use paroxetine for this group of patients. Fluoxetine... may have comparable risks... But you could consider sertraline."*

---

## Section 3 — Assessing PTSD Sleep Disturbance

1. **Investigate nicotine withdrawal in pack-a-day smokers waking after hours. Do not reflexively prescribe quetiapine for this.**
   > *"Is it quetiapine? Some of my colleagues seem to think that's the treatment... because they didn't discover what the actual cause was of why they're awakening."*

2. **Count disturbed awakenings as nightmares for baseline. One remembered plus three disturbed equals four nightmares weekly.**
   > *"a disturbed awakening equals a nightmare... one nightmare a week... and three other nights when they only had a disturbed awakening. Count that as four nightmares per week"*

3. **For sleep terrors with thrashing and screaming, instruct bed partners not to shake the patient awake. Assault risk.**
   > *"if they do try to wake you and shake you, you wake up in a half-awake state... you may attack the person who's trying to wake you"*

---

## Section 4 — Prazosin for PTSD Sleep

1. **Titrate men: 1 mg two nights, 2 mg five nights, then 4, 6, 10, 15, max 20 mg. Median effective 16 mg.**
   > *"1 mg of prazosin for two nights, then... 2 mg for five nights... 4 mg... 6 mg... 10 mg... 15 mg... median dose... was almost 16 mg"*

2. **Titrate women slower: 1 mg two nights, 2 mg fourteen nights, then 4, 6, 10 mg maximum. Median effective 7 mg.**
   > *"1 mg for two nights again but then 2 mg for the rest of two weeks... 4 mg, 6 mg. 10 was the maximum... median dose was 7 for the women."*

3. **Target zero nightmares and zero disturbed awakenings. 90% of VA prazosin prescriptions are underdosed.**
   > *"our goal is to have zero nightmares and disturbed awakenings" / "90% of them were deemed inadequate in that the dosing was nowhere near the evidence-based dosing"*

---

## Section 5 — Sleep Onset Alternatives

1. **Use hydroxyzine first-line for sleep-onset insomnia in PTSD once prazosin has addressed nightmares and awakenings.**
   > *"we consider hydroxyzine our first line with trazodone a second line... we picked hydroxyzine because there is a study by Ahmadpanah finding it effective"*

2. **Trazodone helps sleep onset but does not touch nightmares. Do not expect it to replace prazosin.**
   > *"They're falling asleep with their trazodone but they get the nightmares anyway."*

3. **Screen for conditioned insomnia when fear of nightmares persists. Refer to CBT-I, not more hypnotics.**
   > *"The best treatment for that is cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia, CBT-I... better than adding more pills"*

---

## Section 6 — Sleep Agents to Avoid

1. **Do not use quetiapine first-line for any insomnia. Benefits fail to justify weight, metabolic, cardiac, and TD risks.**
   > *"its benefits did not justify its considerable risks and it should be not be used as a first-line treatment for any kind of insomnia"*

2. **Low hypnotic doses of quetiapine cause equal or greater weight gain than mania doses. Expect ravenous hunger within thirty minutes.**
   > *"the weight gain in quetiapine is not dose related. You're as likely to get it on 25 or 50 mg as you are at the higher doses... the so-called Seroquel munchies"*

3. **Withhold benzodiazepines during exposure therapy. They erase short-term and long-term learning, undermining trauma-focused psychotherapy.**
   > *"It may reduce short- and long-term effectiveness of exposure therapy... They don't remember what they learned from those sessions."*

---

## Section 7 — SSRIs for PTSD

1. **Counsel patients that SSRI benefit in PTSD is modest. Pooled meta-analytic effect size vs placebo is only 0.23.**
   > *"standardized mean difference from placebo in a meta-analysis is 0.23. That's a very small effect size."*

2. **Warn patients about insomnia, sexual dysfunction in 30-60%, and 9-fold GI bleeding risk when combined with NSAIDs.**
   > *"Sexual dysfunction... affecting anywhere from 30% to 60%... Gastrointestinal bleeding can be nine times more common... if they're on... an NSAID"*

3. **Question sertraline in male veterans. It never beat placebo in VA studies; UK approves only for women.**
   > *"It's never beaten placebo as a treatment for VA males... the UK... only chose to approve it in women"*

---

## Section 8 — Augmentation for PTSD with Psychosis

1. **Risperidone has the strongest RCT evidence for PTSD-with-psychosis. A clear trial targeted this subpopulation specifically.**
   > *"Risperidone has the best evidence in that it had a clear-cut randomized controlled trial focusing on this subpopulation exclusively."*

2. **Choose aripiprazole as first antipsychotic augmentation. Fewer side effects than risperidone or quetiapine and probably works.**
   > *"Aripiprazole has fewer side effects, seems to probably work so we do favor it as the first antipsychotic to try"*

3. **Start adjunctive aripiprazole at 2-5 mg. Titrate toward 12 mg per Richardson retrospective.**
   > *"it was started at 2 to 5 mg, titrated up to an average of 12.4 at the end"*

---

## Section 9 — Node 4: SNRIs and Mirtazapine

1. **Do not rely on venlafaxine for hyperarousal, insomnia, or nightmares. Noradrenergic stimulation likely blunts these effects.**
   > *"venlafaxine has this noradrenergic stimulation effect... it may be the reason that venlafaxine did not help hyperarousal or insomnia or nightmares"*

2. **Start duloxetine at 30 mg and titrate toward 120 mg maximum. Expect rapid onset for PTSD plus comorbid depression or anxiety.**
   > *"Duloxetine in a monotherapy trial was started at 30, got to a maximum of 120, PTSD symptoms improved, Hamilton-A improved, rapid onset"*

3. **Use mirtazapine as SSRI augmentation at node 4, not monotherapy. Monotherapy RCT showed no difference from placebo.**
   > *"mirtazapine monotherapy study for PTSD showing no difference from placebo... you could consider it as a choice here at node 4"*

---

## Section 10 — Node 5: Daytime Prazosin

1. **After two failed antidepressants, offer daytime prazosin as the primary third-trial option for persistent hyperarousal.**
   > *"this is the right spot to propose prazosin during the day for your patient who has not responded to two previous SSRI or SNRI trials"*

2. **Target daytime prazosin at hypervigilance in crowds, restaurants, and supermarkets that drives ongoing isolation.**
   > *"hypervigilance in various settings like restaurants and crowds... they don't even want to go to a supermarket... prazosin may be just the thing for the anti-adrenaline effect"*

3. **Dose daytime prazosin according to the 2013 Raskind protocol rather than inventing your own schedule.**
   > *"in the Raskind 2013 study... they gave daytime prazosin"*

---

## Section 11 — Node 5A: Treatment-Resistant PTSD

1. **Skip valproate despite agitation presentation. Two placebo-controlled RCTs show no efficacy for PTSD.**
   > *"two placebo-controlled randomized trials by Davis and Hamner... no efficacy versus placebo for valproate for PTSD"*

2. **Try topiramate around 300 mg at algorithm bottom. Yeh 2011 showed CAPS dropped 58 points versus 32 placebo (p=0.008).**
   > *"CAPS scores went down 58 points in the group getting the topiramate at a typical dose of 300 versus only 32-point drop... with a placebo... p 0.008"*

3. **Show patients the Bonn-Miller cannabis RCT. All three THC and CBD concentrations performed equivalently to placebo.**
   > *"three concentrations of smoked cannabis... All three of the cannabis products had no difference from placebo on PTSD benefit."*

---

## Section 12 — Take-Home Messages

1. **Prescribe prazosin as first-line PTSD medication before SSRIs, reversing the sequence recommended by most competing guidelines.**
   > *"the first-line medication for PTSD would be prazosin, before starting an SSRI which were the generally recommended first-line treatments for PTSD in just about all other guidelines"*

2. **Titrate men weekly: 1 then 2 mg week one, escalating through 4, 6, 10, 15 mg thereafter.**
   > *"1 mg for two nights, 2 mg for five nights to complete the first week. 4 mg for the second week. 6 mg for the third week. 10 mg for the fourth week. 15 mg for the fifth week"*

3. **Reserve SSRIs for after antiadrenergics. Prefer escitalopram, avoid sertraline in men, and weigh paroxetine's side effects.**
   > *"sertraline has never been found to have efficacy in men... paroxetine has more side effects... So we prefer escitalopram."*
